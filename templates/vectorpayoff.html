Evolution of Cooperation in Games of Multiple Contexts with Vector Payoff

As an example, we show individuals in a population engage in games across two contexts in the above figures. In each context, every player employs a specific strategy, which can be either cooperation (C) or defection (D). At each time step, individuals interact with others, and payoffs are calculated in each context based on the corresponding payoff matrices. After the interactions, individuals use non-dominated ranking to update their strategies. Specifically, the focal individual (Bob) will adopt the strategy of a reference individual (Alice or Charlie) if and only if, the reference individual is not worse in all contexts and better in at least one context (denoted by $\succ$; the reverse is denoted by $\prec$). In this example, Bob will imitate Alice, as she has a higher payoff in context 2 and an equal payoff in context 1. However, Bob will not imitate Charlie, since Charlie's payoff is higher in one context but lower in the other (denoted by $\sim$).

Intuition from a Simple Setting

Each block represents a state, with the background color indicating the most dominant strategy in the population. Each arrow represents a possible transition. States with dotted line borders are non-deterministic, indicating they have multiple attractors. States with dashed line borders are deterministic, meaning they have a unique attractor. States with solid line borders belong to closed subsets. The first game is the Prisoner's Dilemma, and the second game is the Snowdrift game. It is possible for the population to fall into gridlock, even when all four strategies are present initially. If the transition indicated by arrow A occurs, strategy DD vanishes, leaving strategy CD and DC as non-comparable, which causes the system to enter a gridlock state. Similarly, if the transition indicated by arrow B occurs, strategy DC vanishes, resulting in a gridlock between strategies CC and DD. Alternatively, the system may enter a closed subset of size two, where perpetual oscillations occur. The payoff matrices are $(R_1, S_1, T_1, P_1) = (1, -0.7, 1.7, 0)$ for the Prisoner's Dilemma game and $(R_2, S_2, T_2, P_2) = (1, 0.875, 1.125, 0)$ for the Snowdrift game.

Visualization: Attraction Basins of Each Closed Subset

  • Red nodes: states belong to closed subsets (attractors).
  • Blue nodes: deterministic states with unique attractors.
  • Green nodes: non-deterministic states with multiple attractors.
  • Edges: possible transitions from transient states to attractors.
  • Labels: population states. For example, $(n_{\text{C}}, n_{\text{D}})$ for single-context games, $(n_{\text{CC}}, n_{\text{CD}}, n_{\text{DC}}, n_{\text{DD}})$ for 2-context games, and $(n_{\text{CCC}}, n_{\text{CCD}}, \ldots, n_{\text{DDD}})$ for 3-context games.
The maximum number of contexts is set to 3 to ensure computational feasibility. The recommended population sizes are 8 or 10 for 2-context games, and 6 for 3-context games.
